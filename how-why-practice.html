Mental health in the midst of the pandemic

The COVID-19 epidemic has had a wide range of effects on us, affecting everything from our social lives to our mental health in some way. Social resistance has also been more widely reported in the media. The epidemic, combined with the rise in social unrest, can be stressful for anybody, but it's critical to recognize how these current events affect communities of color differently.

Despite the fact that mental health issues cause about one-third of all adult disability worldwide, they are frequently under-addressed and neglected in society.

According to data from the Centers for Disease Control (CDC), symptoms of depression were reported 59% more frequently by Hispanic adults (40.3%) than non-Hispanic White adults (25.3%). Additionally, a larger percentage of multi-racial and non-Hispanic adults of other races and ethnicities reported stress and worry about stigma or discrimination associated with being blamed for spreading COVID-19 in comparison to White adults. Among adults with mental illness, 48% of White Americans received mental health services, compared to 31% of Black Americans and Latinx/Hispanic Americans and 22% of AAPI populations.
